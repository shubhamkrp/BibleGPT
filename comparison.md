Short answer: The Bible never mentions a figure named Vishnu. Vishnu is a deity from Hindu tradition, while the biblical texts describe the God of Israel (Yahweh) and, in the New Testament, the person of Jesus Christ. There is no direct counterpart with that name in Scripture.

Who Vishnu is (a quick overview)
Aspect	Hindu tradition
Role	One of the three principal deities of the Trimurti (Brahma‑the creator, Vishnu‑the preserver, Shiva‑the destroyer).
Key attributes	Maintains and protects the universe; embodies compassion and righteousness.
Avatars	In the Vaishnava tradition, Vishnu is believed to descend to Earth in “avatars” (incarnations) to restore dharma. The most famous are Krishna, Rama, Narasimha, Vamana, etc.
Scriptural sources	Vedas, Upanishads, Bhagavad‑Gītā, Mahābhārata, Puranas (especially the Vishnu‑Purāna).
Worship	Central to many Hindu sects (Vaishnavism). Devotees chant “Om Namo Vishnave” and celebrate festivals such as Vaikunta Ekadashi.
Why you won’t find Vishnu in the Bible
Different religious traditions – The Bible is the sacred scripture of Judaism and Christianity, developed in the ancient Near East, whereas Vishnu belongs to the Indian religious milieu.

Monotheistic focus – The biblical worldview is monotheistic: it affirms one sovereign God (Yahweh/Jesus) rather than a pantheon of deities with specialized functions.

No textual overlap – The Hebrew Bible (Old Testament) and the Christian New Testament contain no references to Hindu deities, mythology, or terminology such as “Vishnu,” “avatar,” or “Trimurti.”

If you’re looking for a “similar role” in the Bible
Many people wonder whether a biblical figure “does the job” that Vishnu does in Hinduism—namely, preserve and sustain creation. Here are a few ways Christians might think about that:

Biblical concept	How it relates (and differs)
God the Father (Yahweh)	Described as the creator and sovereign ruler who “sustains all things” (Heb. “holds all things together” – Colossians 1:17). In the biblical worldview, the same God who created also preserves, so there isn’t a separate “preserver” deity.
Jesus Christ	In Christian theology, Jesus is the incarnate Son of God who entered history to redeem humanity (John 1:14; Philippians 2:6‑11). Some see a parallel to Vishnu’s avatars, but the Christian claim is that Christ is the unique, one‑time, all‑sufficient incarnation of the one true God, not one of many periodic descendents.
The Holy Spirit	Often described as the “breath of God” that continually sustains life (Psalm 104:30; Acts 2:17‑18). The Spirit’s ongoing presence could be loosely likened to a sustaining power, but again it’s not a distinct deity separate from the Father and Son.
Divine providence	Passages such as Romans 8:28 (“all things work together for good”) and Psalm 23 (the Shepherd who “provides”) reflect the biblical view that God actively cares for and upholds creation.
Key difference: In the Bible, all these functions—creation, preservation, redemption—are ultimately attributes of the same one God, expressed in the doctrine of the Trinity (Father, Son, Holy Spirit). In Hinduism, especially in the Vaishnava tradition, Vishnu is one distinct aspect of the divine reality, separate from Brahma (creator) and Shiva (destroyer), though they are all ultimately part of a larger cosmic principle.

Comparative notes (for curiosity, not theological equivalence)
Feature	Vishnu (Hinduism)	God/Christ (Christianity)

Divine name	“Vishnu” (the all‑pervading)	“Yahweh,” “Elohim,” “Lord,” “God,” “Jesus.”

These parallels are illustrative and do not imply that Vishnu appears in biblical texts or that Christians consider Vishnu a biblical figure.

Bottom line
Vishnu is a Hindu deity; he does not appear in the Bible.

The biblical God (Yahweh) fulfills the role of creator, sustainer, and redeemer all by Himself, and Christians understand Jesus Christ as the unique incarnation of that same God.
